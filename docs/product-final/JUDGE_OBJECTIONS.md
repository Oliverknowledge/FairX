# Judge objections — direct answers

## Why does this need blockchain?

The sequence rule can exist in a centralized database. The blockchain is justified by the money and evidence boundary: accepted liabilities reserve program-controlled collateral; returned orders cannot acquire a durable claim; claims and operator withdrawal obey the same public state; and outsiders can recompute the lifecycle without trusting the operator's ledger or this frontend. FairX should not claim that blockchain is required to compare two integers.

## Why not just pause the market?

Pausing prevents stale execution but removes access for synchronized traders. FairX rejects only an ineligible old-sequence order and allows the next synchronized order to execute. That selective continuity is the product.

## Why not copy Polymarket?

FairX is not a new consumer exchange and does not claim to beat Polymarket on liquidity, discovery, or network effects. It is a reference execution primitive an operator can add to centrally quoted live markets where official event state can outrun the quote. Its hackathon differentiation is TxLINE-bound eligibility plus publicly reconcilable Solana liabilities.

## Who pays?

The intended buyer is a live prediction-market operator that bears stale-price liability and needs an auditable cancellation/settlement policy. A commercial model is not proven. Plausible pricing is infrastructure SaaS plus per-settlement fees, but the submission has no fabricated customer, revenue, or volume claim.

## What exactly integrates?

The operator keeps discovery, pricing, frontend, user acquisition, and compliance. It binds a market to TxLINE fixture evidence, publishes an executable quote with the latest material-event sequence, submits orders through V4 eligibility, and uses program-constrained resolution/reconciliation.

## What is still trusted?

TxLINE source evidence; the operator's pricing authority and feed submission; two of three configured resolution authorities; service availability; and the current single-key upgrade authority. The prototype is unaudited and deployed only to devnet.

## What if TxLINE is wrong or unavailable?

FairX cannot make bad source evidence true. TxLINE is an explicit oracle dependency. Availability policy—pause, degrade, or reject—is still operator-owned and is not solved by the current prototype.

## Is the quote fair?

FairX does not attest that the operator-set probability, spread, or pricing model is fair. It proves only whether the order was bound to the required sequence and then enforces the resulting fixed liability.

## Is it really a refund?

The old-sequence instruction returns the submitted 0.01 SOL principal and creates no position liability. Solana network transaction fees are separate and are not reimbursed. Product copy therefore uses “principal returned,” not “full refund.”

## Is the replay live?

No. It is a deterministic historical replay of genuine France–Morocco TxLINE evidence and finalized V4 state. It sends no transactions. The deployed program and 24-transaction lifecycle are verified separately on `/proof`.

## What does the economic number prove?

For the canonical 0.01 SOL YES order, accepting 53.28% and later paying a winning fixed payout would create 0.008769297 SOL of operator liability. The synchronized 87.48% order creates 0.001431275 SOL. The former is 6.13 times the latter. This is a transparent counterfactual from the recorded prices and payout formula—not observed loss, aggregate savings, volume, or traction.

## What disappears if FairX exists?

The operator no longer has to choose between accepting an order tied to obsolete event state, pausing every synchronized trader, or asking users to trust a private discretionary cancellation. FairX does not make oracle, pricing, compliance, governance, or market-demand problems disappear.
